Jason Becker’s brilliant tweet 🐦 was born of a thread that began with Jesse Megan reflecting on educational philosophies and trade-offs.

Jesse has been leading an “R for Data Science” online learning community (in its second week of operation, as of this writing). And, amid the R4DS-community activity, and Jason’s tweet, I got to thinking about the fact that this kind of expertise– the kind where you know the words to search for– is not, in fact, insubstantial.

Rather than opine on the nature of knowledge, allow me to offer a “just so” story of sorts about a time when I made a mistake that I couldn’t Google my way out of.1

Mara makes a very stupid mistake

Long long ago, when I was young and naïve,2 I was trying to learn something on the computer that involved my using Vagrant, a virtual dev environment software. I wasn’t trying to do anything fancy; the goal was literally just to start running a Vagrant virtual machine, and then stop running it, but I hadn’t done anything at the command line for a very long time.

It all seemed so simple. To get going, I just needed to run vagrant up in the right directory. You can see what this should look like below:3

To stop the virtual environment, you run vagrant halt. Easy enough, right? Well…

I could not get it to work. I read and re-read the documentation, looked at answers on StackOverflow, went through the whole thing on a computer isn’t working right routine many of us know so well. As it turned out, the problem was that I was trying to “halt” vagrant from within vagrant.

It’s a difficult thing to describe using words (See? I’m circling back to my point!), but I was, in effect, doing the vagrant-equivalent of trying to quit Microsoft Word by typing out the word "quit" (see visual aid below).

Of course, if this analogy had been apparent to me at the time, none of this would have occurred. That’s what makes the curse of knowledge oh-so vexing: in order to make this analogy, you have to understand what’s going on, but it’s only because you don’t understand what’s going on that you need this analogy at all.

Innocence lost

I’ve been re-reading The Design of Everyday Things by Donald A. Norman, and I’ll close with a passage that I think captures the spirit of this quite well.

“Innocence lost is not easily regained. The designer simply cannot predict the problems people will have, the misinterpretations that will arise, the errors that will get made.” — Donald A. Norman, The Design of Everyday Things
